Conflict

Lock 4 People in a Van for 3 Months

January 25, 2015

In my former life, I played drums in a touring rock band. A band is a close-knit group of people who live and travel in close quarters and as a result, there can be a lot of chaos and conflict.

To add further complexity, my band included my sister, her husband and a roving cast of bass players. So, take the normal level of complexity and add family dynamics to it!

There were numerous times when the stress and frustration was overwhelming. Over the 10 years that I played with the band, I had to develop a number of techniques to deal with conflict (other than just hitting the drums really hard).

When we first starting making records, we had a knock-down drag-out fight during a recording session over the way I was playing something. There was a drum part that I was particularly attached to that got changed by my band mates. I was not happy about it.

I felt that I was the drummer and that I knew what the perfect drum part should be. On a deeper level, I felt that my ability and musical taste were being called into question.

As with any group, there were constant power struggles and I felt that the band was taking away my power.

To diffuse the situation, I decided to take a break and get some fresh air. During my walk, I was able to calm down and reflect that my reaction had nothing to do with the process of making music. It had to do with my ego!

Whether or not the drum part seemed perfect to me, it was not connecting to the songwriter and thus not conveying the intent or spirit of the song.

This was the beginning of a new awareness for me. It centered around the question: Why are we all sitting in this room? The answer was to create music. It was not for me to be proven right or to show off. It was to collaborate and to create something awesome.

Music, like any creative process, involves vision and determination, but also compromise and empathy.

From that point on, I made a conscious effort to be open to everyoneâ€™s ideas. I made a point of both expressing my ideas and not reacting negatively if the ideas were not received.

The process of recording was much more enjoyable when I adopted that mindset and I was constantly ending up in new exciting places musically.
